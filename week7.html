Week 7 Blog – Second-Cycle Prototyping & Spatial Development

Published by 1 May 2026

The Experience

The focus this week was on moving forward with the second round of experimentation following the first critique session. After receiving feedback in Week 6, I began to de-emphasize the overall Eco Hub concept and shifted my focus more directly to spatial interaction, participation, and realism.

My main task this week was to develop the Eco Hub prototype using Blender. I started with a very simple spatial blockout to test the layout, circulation, and overall composition. At this stage, I deliberately kept the prototype simple because I wanted to focus on spatial relationships rather than visual polish.

After completing the basic layout, I began gradually adding more elements, such as seating areas, shelter spaces, and ecological boundaries. This allowed me to start testing how users might move through the space and where interactions were most likely to occur.

The final phase of development this week involved adding vegetation and environmental elements. Trees, shrubs, and planting borders make the Eco Hub more immersive and better align with the project’s ecological themes.

I didn’t treat this prototype as a finished architectural rendering, but rather as a tool for testing participation and environmental engagement.

Reflection on Action

The biggest change this week is that I’ve stopped viewing Eco Hub as just an “idea” and now see it as a spatial system that needs to be tested in practice.

Over the past few weeks, I’ve mainly focused on explaining the concept itself and the relationships between business, ecology, and climate resilience. But when I actually started modeling in Blender, I realized that many important issues can only truly be understood through spatial testing.

For example:

  • Does the space feel inviting?
  • Is the seating layout practical?
  • Does the shelter create interaction or only decoration?
  • Is the ecological area visually integrated or separated?
  • Does the scale feel realistic?

These questions became much more visible once the project entered three-dimensional space.

This week, I also realized that I still tend to prioritize visual atmosphere right away. As soon as I started adding trees and vegetation, the entire project immediately felt more complete and visually appealing. But I also realized that visual improvements don’t necessarily mean the experiment itself is any stronger.

This reflection helped me become more aware of the difference between visual resolution and conceptual clarity

Theory

Week 7 strongly emphasised that blogs should function as process documentation rather than simply displaying final outcomes. The lecture repeatedly encouraged students to show “messy making,” including rough sketches, failed attempts, iterations, and process development.

This directly connected to my modelling process this week. Instead of only presenting the final render, I began documenting:

  • blockout stages
  • structure development
  • vegetation testing
  • layout adjustments

The class also reiterated that the purpose of experiments is not to immediately resolve the entire brief, but rather to test uncertainties through iteration and build understanding.

This helped me rethink the significance of prototyping. I realized that the Eco Hub model was not intended to “prove” the final project, but rather to test whether participation and environmental engagement could truly emerge through spatial intervention.

The class also placed particular emphasis on reflective judgment, rather than mere description. Reflection should explain why certain decisions were made and how experimentation has altered our understanding.

This is especially important in my project, as the modeling process itself has already begun to shift my understanding of “what to test next.”

Preparation

This week taught me that spatial modelling can reveal problems and opportunities that are difficult to notice in sketches alone.

The next stage of the project needs to focus more specifically on:

  • interaction
  • participation behaviour
  • user engagement
  • realistic scale
  • environmental usability

Rather than simply improving visual quality, I want the next experiment to test whether people can clearly understand how the Eco Hub is intended to function.

For Week 8, my goals are:

  • refine the project framing further
  • test participation scenarios more clearly
  • explore how businesses would realistically engage with the space
  • prepare stronger experimentation goals for Crit Session 2
  • continue documenting process development instead of only final outcomes

The most important lesson this week was understanding that modelling is not only visual communication — it is also a way of thinking through spatial behaviour and testing uncertainty.
