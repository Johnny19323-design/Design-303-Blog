Week 1: Identifying skills, interests, and demo ideas

Published by 06 March 2026

1.What I did this week

This week, I’ve been focusing on understanding the course structure and thinking about what kind of technical demo I can present in Week 3. From Week 1, I’ve learned that this course is designed as a space for self-directed exploration, where we’re expected to explore ideas, test methods, and improve our technical skills throughout the process, while documenting and reflecting on our progress through weekly blog posts. I believe this blog will help me track my thoughts, challenges, and growth as a designer.

In class, we were also asked to reflect on our existing skills and brainstorm potential directions for tech demos with our classmates. Since the first blog post is meant to document these ideas, reflections, and key takeaways, the most important task for me this week was to identify my current strengths and determine which direction is both realistic and worth pursuing further.

2.Ideas I considered

At first, I had three main ideas in mind: using Blender for 3D modeling, using Aseprite for pixel art, and writing code for game design. I’m actually quite drawn to game design because coding allows for a high degree of interactivity, and it’s also well-suited for future design projects. However, after giving it some serious thought, I realized that creating a game demo through coding would take too much time. It also requires a significant amount of upfront planning, debugging, and testing, which might not be practical for a small, preliminary classroom demo.

The second area is Aseprite pixel art. I really like the visual style of pixel art, and it’s well-suited for animation and game-related projects. However, I have almost no experience in this field, so I think it will be difficult to get the hang of it in a short period of time. The challenge isn’t just learning how to use the software, but also adapting to a new visual logic and animation style at the same time.

The third area is 3D modeling. Compared to the previous two, this area suits me better because I already have some basic knowledge in this field. Although I’m not yet very proficient, it’s not entirely unfamiliar to me, which makes me feel that it strikes a better balance between challenge and feasibility.

3.Why I chose three-dimensional modelling

In the end, I chose 3D modeling as my focus and decided to use Blender as my primary tool. The main reason is that this path is the most practical option for me at this stage. It aligns with my existing interests, and it’s a skill I can continue to improve through consistent practice. It will also be very helpful for future work in prototyping, visual communication, and concept development.

Another reason is that this course is designed to encourage us to identify skills we already have some foundation in and develop them into a demo that can be helpful to our classmates. As mentioned in the first week’s lecture materials, the purpose of a tech demo is to introduce classmates to a technology, material, or method that may be useful for future prototyping and experimentation. 3D modeling fits this purpose well because it can not only be used to express ideas but also to more directly showcase structure and appearance, thereby conveying concepts more clearly.

4.Tool investigation

Blender

Although I’ve decided to pursue 3D modeling, I also realize that choosing the right software is a crucial decision in itself. There are many software programs available for 3D modeling, and each one works differently. Blender is the tool I ultimately decided to focus on. Its main advantage is that it offers a comprehensive set of features, covering various stages such as modeling, texturing, lighting, rendering, and even animation. Plus, since it’s widely used by students and independent creators, there are plenty of tutorials and learning resources available. For me, the appeal of Blender lies in the fact that I can explore a wide range of possibilities using a single tool, rather than having to switch between multiple software programs right from the start.

Aseprite

Aseprite is well-suited for pixel art and simple frame-by-frame animation, as well as for creating stylized visuals and small animation clips. However, it has a fairly niche focus, and it doesn’t quite align with my current experience or the direction of the demo I’m planning to create.

Game engines and coding tools

Game-related coding tools or engines have the advantage of enabling the creation of interactive, systematic, and playable experiences, and they offer a high degree of flexibility. However, for me, the biggest drawback of development right now is that it’s too time-consuming. Because these tools typically require me to tackle both design thinking and technical issues simultaneously, they can make an early-stage demo very cumbersome.
