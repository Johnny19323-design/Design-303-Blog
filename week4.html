Week 4 Blog – Brief Scoping, Experiment Ideation, and First Experiment

Published by 27 March 2026

The Experience

This week, I began to move from broader ideas towards a more specific project direction, focusing on the Climate Resilience brief. My brief is in the Business stream; at first, I found this direction rather challenging, as my previous interests and practice had mainly centred on 3D modelling in Blender, whereas the Business stream leans more towards sustainability systems, institutions and social issues. During the brief scoping process, I realised that ‘business’ is not merely about making money or commercial activities per se. This brief focuses more on climate resilience, placemaking, ecological relationships, and how businesses and organisations can become part of the local ecosystem rather than existing in isolation. It also explores how local organisations can foster a sense of belonging, circular systems and nature-positive relationships. This has transformed my understanding of the project.

Through the ‘reverse brief’ exercise, I began to clarify exactly what I wanted to focus on, and what should be left outside the scope. I discovered that my strongest connection to this brief lies in spatial design and visual communication. Rather than attempting to tackle the entire business system, I am more interested in exploring a small-scale physical intervention to see if it can make sustainability more visible and accessible. This led me to my first experimental direction: an Eco Hub, which is a small shared ecological space situated within a commercial area. This Eco Hub could include a compost station, a rain garden, an area for native plants, a shared relaxation area, and an information board for local engagement. I plan to use sketches and preliminary 3D modelling to test whether this kind of spatial intervention can help foster stronger connections between business, place and ecology.

My first formal experiment focused on testing spatial intervention rather than directly completing the final project. In accordance with the Experiment Planning Guide, I first defined the experiment’s focus, method and success criteria before moving on to the modelling stage. Of course, the course also emphasised that an early prototype should be small and focused, rather than a complete final solution.

Reflection on Action

My aim this time is not simply to create a visually appealing 3D model, but to use modelling as a testing tool to visualise a business problem that would otherwise remain invisible. I chose this approach because spatial thinking is one of the design methods I’m most comfortable with. When I can explain an idea through form, layout and visual structure, I feel more confident than when relying solely on words. At the same time, Blender is a tool I already have some experience with, so it’s the most practical starting point for now. However, during the planning process, I came across a significant issue: I’m actually not entirely sure what I’m testing. Am I testing the physical space itself? Or am I testing participation, service systems, and the relationships between people and the environment? This uncertainty needs to be taken into account.

This made me realise that I needed a clearer focus. If a prototype tries to test everything at once, it becomes too broad and difficult to evaluate. I began to understand that a more robust experiment stems from testing just one key aspect, rather than the entire system. I also noticed that my comfort zone was influencing my decisions. As I am more accustomed to modelling, I naturally tend to prioritise spatial solutions. This helps me get started more quickly, but it also means I might be avoiding service design or systems thinking simply because they are not familiar enough to me.

This reflection has also led me to think more seriously about positionality. My background influences how I define problems, and it also affects which solutions feel more natural to me.

Theory

The Integrated Reflective Cycle is far more effective than Gibbs’ Reflective Cycle in helping me organise the events of the week. It doesn’t just allow me to describe what happened, but encourages me to link together experience, theory, reflection and future preparation. It also prompts me to pay closer attention to context and positionality.

One key point that came across in class was that DES303 serves as a testing ground for experimentation. The focus is not on protecting a perfect, final idea, but rather on testing early, identifying issues early, and gathering information early. This has taken a lot of the pressure off me. I realised that I don’t need to resolve the entire business stream straight away; I just need to test the Eco Hub concept first to see whether it can effectively convey the relationship between business and ecology.

The Experiment Planning Guide also emphasises the importance of clarifying the focus of an experiment by asking: ‘I want to explore focus by action to better understand the goal’. This framework has helped me narrow down the scope of my experiment and avoid turning it into an overly complex final project.

Preparation

This week has made me realise that one of my strengths as a designer is the ability to use visual methods to make abstract concepts easier to understand. When I think through sketches, layouts and models, I feel more confident than when I simply write about theory. At the same time, I’ve realised that I need to be careful not to let my comfort zone limit the direction of my projects. Prioritising modelling has been helpful, but I still need to constantly consider whether this is the most appropriate approach for the problem at hand.

For Week 5, my next steps are:

  • create clearer concept sketches for the Eco Hub
  • make an early rough three-dimensional prototype in Blender
  • simplify the experiment focus so I only test one key quality
  • prepare stronger feedback questions for the upcoming critique session
  • compare whether spatial intervention is stronger than service-based directions
